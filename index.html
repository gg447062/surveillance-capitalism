What is Fingerprinting?

Every time you visit a website, your browser quietly hands over dozens of tiny details about your device: your screen size, the fonts you have installed, your time zone, your browser version, and much more. Individually, these details seem harmless. Together, they form a unique picture of your device, a "fingerprint," that can identify you across the web without you ever knowing.

This is called digital fingerprinting, and it is one of the most powerful tracking tools in use today. Unlike cookies, it requires no consent, leaves no trace you can delete, and works even if you use incognito mode. Tracking companies use it to follow users across websites, target them with ads, and sell that data to brokers. Digital advertising is a business worth hundreds of billions of dollars, and your fingerprint is part of what fuels it.

How it works:

Fingerprinting comes in two main forms. Browser fingerprinting collects information through your browser when you visit a site. Device fingerprinting collects it through apps installed on your device. In both cases, the process happens silently in the background, without any notification or opt-in.

Top third-party web domains

When you visit a website, you are rarely just talking to that one site. Most pages secretly load dozens of outside entities in the background, without you ever seeing them. These are called third-party trackers, and they follow you from site to site, building a detailed profile of your behavior and your interests.

This dataset, built by DuckDuckGo, maps exactly who those hidden guests are. It tracks the most common third-party domains across the web, and records how they behave: how many sites they appear on, whether they use fingerprinting, whether they drop cookies, and who owns them.

Why Google deserves its own chart

When we looked at the full picture, one name stood so far above the rest that it changed the scale of everything else. Google appears on 67% of all websites crawled in this dataset. That means roughly two out of every three pages you visit are quietly reporting back to Google, regardless of whether you use any Google product or ever clicked on a Google ad.

Google also scores a 3 out of 3 on fingerprinting, the maximum level. (If *any* of an owner's domains scores a 3, it scores a 3. The logic being: the worst behavior defines how invasive they are.) It is not just the most widespread tracker on the web. It is also one of the most invasive.

To understand the scale of how Google operates, we pulled its domains out separately. The chart below shows the individual Google-owned domains that appear across the web, and what each one does. Some are familiar (Google Fonts, YouTube). Others are invisible infrastructure built entirely for tracking and advertising. Together, they form a surveillance network embedded into the web itself.

The indices measured are:

Prevalence: how much of the web this domain appears on. A prevalence of 0.9 means it shows up on 90% of websites you visit. Google fonts, for example, is basically everywhere.

Fingerprinting: a sneaky tracking technique that doesn't use cookies. Instead it identifies your browser by tiny details (screen size, fonts installed, etc.) to recognize you across sites. Scored 0–3, higher = more aggressive.

Cookies: the classic tracking method. How many cookies this domain drops on your browser.

Owner/Entity: the parent company. Dozens of different-looking domains often belong to the same giant (e.g. Google owns hundreds of tracking domains).

Categories: what the domain is *for*: Advertising, Analytics, Social, CDN, etc.

Sites: The raw number of websites this domain was found on.

Why trust DuckDuckGo's dataset

DuckDuckGo is a software company that focuses on online privacy. Their browser offers protections from ad tracking, cookies and even brower fingerprinting. This dataset is the one they use for blocking trackers and is "automatically generated, constantly updated, and continually tested." DuckDuckGo published this data for the benefit of the public as well as to further their mission of increased internet privacy.

If you would like to know more, you can read their blog post about the data set.

What can you do about it?

Fingerprinting is hard to escape, but not impossible to fight back against. Using a privacy-focused browser like Firefox or Tor, installing tracker-blocking extensions, and avoiding unnecessary apps are all steps that make it harder to be fingerprinted.
